üìã Project Overview

The Logistics Risk Predictor is a machine learning-powered web application designed to predict delivery delays in e-commerce logistics. Built using real-world data from Brazilian e-commerce transactions, this project combines advanced machine learning techniques with Geographic Information Systems (GIS) to provide accurate risk assessments for order deliveries.

The system analyzes multiple factors including product characteristics (weight, dimensions, price), geographic locations (seller and customer states), temporal patterns (purchase month, day, hour), and freight values to predict whether an order will be delivered on time or experience delays.

üí° Project Idea: Logistics Risk Predictor

Logistics & GIS-Based Risk Assessment

This project combines Logistics Analytics and Geographic Information Systems (GIS) to create a predictive model for delivery delays. By analyzing spatial relationships between seller and customer locations, product characteristics, and temporal patterns, the system provides actionable insights for logistics planning and risk management.

The model uses a Random Forest Classifier trained on historical delivery data to predict the probability of delivery delays. Key features include:

  • Product Features: Price, weight, dimensions, volume, and density
  • Geographic Features: Seller and customer states, calculated delivery distance
  • Temporal Features: Purchase month, day of week, and hour
  • Logistics Features: Freight value and shipping characteristics

üåç Haversine Distance Formula

The delivery distance between seller and customer locations is calculated using the Haversine formula, a mathematical equation that determines the great-circle distance between two points on a sphere given their latitude and longitude coordinates.

Why Haversine instead of other distance formulas?

  • Spherical Geometry: The Earth is approximately spherical, and the Haversine formula accounts for the Earth's curvature, providing accurate distance calculations over long distances. Simple Euclidean distance (straight-line) would be inaccurate for geographic coordinates.
  • Accuracy for Long Distances: For Brazil's vast geographic expanse (over 8.5 million km¬≤), calculating distances between states requires accounting for the Earth's curvature. The Haversine formula provides accurate results even for distances spanning thousands of kilometers.
  • Standard Practice: Haversine is the industry standard for calculating distances between geographic coordinates in GIS and logistics applications, making it the appropriate choice for this project.
  • Computational Efficiency: While more complex than Euclidean distance, Haversine is computationally efficient and provides the necessary accuracy for logistics predictions without excessive computational overhead.

The formula calculates the shortest distance along the Earth's surface (great-circle distance), which is essential for accurate delivery time predictions, as actual shipping routes follow the Earth's curvature rather than straight lines.

üåé Why This Project Matters: The Brazilian Context

Brazil presents unique challenges for e-commerce logistics that make predictive delivery risk assessment particularly valuable:

Geographic Complexity

Brazil is the fifth-largest country in the world, spanning over 8.5 million square kilometers. The vast distances between regions, combined with diverse terrain (Amazon rainforest, mountains, coastal areas), create significant logistical challenges. Delivery times can vary dramatically depending on the origin and destination states.

Infrastructure Challenges

Despite being a major economy, Brazil faces infrastructure challenges including:

  • Inconsistent road quality across different regions
  • Limited access to remote areas, especially in the Amazon region
  • Seasonal weather impacts (rainy seasons affecting transportation)
  • Varying levels of logistics infrastructure development across states

Economic Impact

Late deliveries have significant economic consequences:

  • Customer Satisfaction: Delayed deliveries lead to customer complaints and reduced trust
  • Operational Costs: Failed deliveries require redelivery attempts, increasing logistics costs
  • Business Reputation: Poor delivery performance affects marketplace ratings and seller reputation
  • Inventory Management: Unpredictable delivery times complicate inventory planning

By predicting delivery delays, businesses can:

  • Set realistic customer expectations with accurate delivery estimates
  • Optimize logistics routes and carrier selection
  • Implement proactive measures for high-risk orders
  • Improve overall customer satisfaction and reduce operational costs

Model Comparison: Random Forest vs. XGBoost

Two machine learning algorithms were evaluated for this project: Random Forest and XGBoost. Both models showed competitive performance, with Random Forest achieving slightly better results and being selected as the production model.

Random Forest Confusion Matrix

Performance Metrics:

  • Accuracy: 93%
  • ROC-AUC Score: 0.7898
  • Precision (Late Orders): 87% - When the model predicts a delay, it's correct 87% of the time
  • Recall (Late Orders): 15% - The model catches 15% of all actual late orders

The Random Forest model demonstrates high precision, meaning when it flags an order as potentially late, it's highly accurate. However, it has lower recall, indicating it may miss some late orders. This trade-off prioritizes precision over recall, which is valuable for minimizing false alarms while still identifying high-risk orders.

XGBoost Confusion Matrix

Performance Metrics:

  • Accuracy: 92%
  • ROC-AUC Score: 0.7706
  • Precision (Late Orders): 63% - When the model predicts a delay, it's correct 63% of the time
  • Recall (Late Orders): 7% - The model catches 7% of all actual late orders

The XGBoost model shows slightly lower overall performance but has different characteristics. It has lower precision and recall for late orders compared to Random Forest, but it's generally faster to train and results in smaller model file sizes.

Why Random Forest Was Chosen

While both models showed similar ROC-AUC scores (0.79 vs. 0.77), Random Forest was selected as the production model for the following reasons:

  • Superior Performance: Random Forest achieved higher accuracy (93% vs. 92%) and a better ROC-AUC score (0.7898 vs. 0.7706), indicating better overall predictive capability
  • Higher Precision: With 87% precision for late orders, Random Forest provides more reliable predictions when flagging potential delays, reducing false positives
  • Better Recall: While both models have relatively low recall, Random Forest's 15% recall is significantly better than XGBoost's 7%, meaning it catches more actual late orders
  • Interpretability: Random Forest models are generally more interpretable, making it easier to understand feature importance and model decisions

When to Consider XGBoost

Although Random Forest performed best in this project, XGBoost can be a viable alternative when model performances are close, particularly in production environments:

  • Faster Inference: XGBoost models are generally faster to run during prediction, which can improve Flask app responsiveness, especially under high traffic
  • Smaller File Size: XGBoost models typically result in smaller .pkl file sizes, reducing memory footprint and deployment complexity
  • Scalability: For larger datasets or when retraining frequently, XGBoost's training speed can be advantageous
  • Trade-off Analysis: If the ROC-AUC scores are very close (within 0.01-0.02), the operational benefits of XGBoost (speed, file size) might outweigh the slight performance difference, depending on the specific use case requirements

Decision Framework: When choosing between models with similar performance, consider whether you prioritize catching every late order (higher recall) or being extremely accurate when flagging one (higher precision). For this project, precision was prioritized to minimize false alarms while maintaining reasonable recall, making Random Forest the optimal choice.
